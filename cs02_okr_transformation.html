01 — The Situation

Flying
Blind

When I took over the medical imaging viewers portfolio, the teams were talented and genuinely busy — but operating in silos. Strong people, broken system.

Symptom 01

Each team tracked different metrics — velocity, bug counts, release numbers. None connected to clinical or business outcomes.

Symptom 02

Mid-quarter roadmap churn was constant. Priorities shifted reactively, with no shared framework to make trade-offs explicit.

Symptom 03

Two teams were solving the same image-load problem from different ends — without knowing it. Duplicated work, wasted capacity.

Symptom 04

Siloed architectural decisions during a major cloud modernization — teams making well-intentioned but uncoordinated choices.

Symptom 05

Leadership couldn't answer: "How does this feature help a radiologist read faster?" — no clear line of sight from work to outcome.

Symptom 06

Major enterprise renewals upcoming. Competitors marketing AI-ready and cloud-native capabilities. Urgency was high and rising.

We're doing good work, but it feels like we're flying blind.

— Consistent feedback from engineering and product teams during sprint reviews
02 — The Diagnosis

The Problem
Behind the
Problem

The issue wasn't team capability. It was a missing operating system. Activity was high — alignment was absent. The gap between work and outcomes was invisible, and that invisibility was expensive.

What teams were measuring
—Sprint velocity and completion rates
—Bug counts and ticket closures
—Number of features released
—Release frequency
What they should have been measuring
+Diagnostic report turnaround time
+Viewer adoption across care teams
+Revenue retention and renewal rates
+Delivery predictability against commitments

The reframe: This wasn't a prioritization problem or a planning problem. It was a connection problem. Teams couldn't see how their work reached a clinician. Once I named that gap, fixing it became straightforward — not easy, but clear.

03 — My Approach

How I
Fixed It

I didn't start with frameworks. I started by listening. Then I built a system that connected every decision to an outcome — without creating bureaucracy.

01

Diagnosed before prescribing

Sat in on sprint demos, roadmap reviews, and customer calls for two weeks before proposing anything. The pattern was consistent: teams were optimizing locally, no one had a portfolio-level view. That clarity shaped everything that followed.

02

Co-created OKRs anchored to real-world outcomes

Led cross-functional workshops with product, engineering, and clinical leaders to build OKRs from the ground up — not handed down, built together. Every OKR had to pass one test: does this connect to something a clinician or a customer actually experiences?

03

Standardized prioritization with RICE across the portfolio

Replaced subjective roadmap debates with a consistent scoring framework. Every initiative evaluated on reach, impact, confidence, and effort — same lens, every team. Trade-offs became explicit and defensible instead of political.

04

Built a roadmap governance layer in Aha

Every initiative had to declare which OKR it served and which enterprise metric it moved. Built Aha dashboards that gave leadership real-time visibility — not just what was being built, but why and how it connected to portfolio outcomes.

05

Introduced a bi-weekly release readiness cadence

Replaced ad-hoc release coordination with a structured cadence using clear RACI ownership. Engineering, QA, product, and release ops aligned every two weeks — not at the end of the sprint when surprises had already accumulated.

06

Embedded analytics into every planning ritual

Integrated Mixpanel and internal telemetry into viewer products. Every planning meeting opened with data — adoption rates, load times, error rates, support ticket patterns. The question shifted from "what should we build?" to "what does the evidence say we should build?"

The reframe — OKR example
Before — activity-based
Objective: Deliver PACS 3.2 release on schedule
Key Result: Complete 8 features from the backlog
After — outcome-based
Objective: Accelerate clinical decision-making in high-volume radiology environments
Key Result: Reduce average diagnostic report turnaround time by 15% across enterprise accounts

That subtle shift reframed every team conversation from task completion to clinical impact. Engineering could finally answer the question leadership kept asking: "How does this help a radiologist?"

04 — Key Decisions

Where I
Chose

Decision 01

Listen first, build nothing for two weeks

There was pressure to fix things quickly — the modernization was in motion and renewals were coming. I pushed back and invested two weeks purely in observation. Without that diagnostic phase, I'd have prescribed the wrong treatment. The patterns I found shaped the entire operating model that followed.

Decision 02

Build OKRs with teams, not for them

I could have rolled out a framework top-down. Instead, I ran workshops where each team proposed their own key results within the portfolio objectives. It was slower, messier, and occasionally uncomfortable — but it produced genuine ownership. Teams didn't just know the OKRs, they believed in them. That's the only kind of OKR that changes behavior.

Decision 03

Make the OKR workshops about trade-offs, not targets

The most important question in every planning session wasn't "what do we commit to?" — it was "if we commit to this, what do we say no to?" That reframe made teams think seriously about capacity and dependencies. It also reduced mid-quarter surprises because the hard conversations happened upfront.

Decision 04

Celebrate course-correction, not just delivery

This was a deliberate culture choice. I didn't want teams gaming metrics to look good — I wanted teams that would escalate early when something wasn't working. So I explicitly recognized teams who changed direction based on data, not just those who hit their targets. That shift in what gets celebrated changes what people do when no one's watching.

Decision 05

Keep the governance lean enough to actually use

Every process I introduced had to earn its place. I killed anything that created overhead without insight. The test: does this help a team member make a better decision, or does it just produce a report for leadership? Anything that only served the latter got cut.

06 — Why It Worked

Why It
Worked

Operational excellence isn't about control — it's about clarity. When people see the connection between their work and a real outcome, alignment becomes natural.

01

Diagnose before prescribing

Two weeks of listening revealed patterns that two years of retrospectives had missed. The quality of your diagnosis determines the quality of your intervention. Most operational problems are treated before they're understood.

02

OKRs are an alignment tool, not a reporting artifact

The value of an OKR isn't the document — it's the conversation that produced it. The trade-off discussions, the forced prioritization, the shared language. If your OKRs don't create productive conflict during planning, they're not working.

03

Connect individual work to end outcomes explicitly

Engineers who can answer "how does my feature help a radiologist?" make different decisions than engineers who can't. That connection doesn't happen on its own — it has to be built deliberately into how work is framed, discussed, and evaluated.

04

Governance has to earn its place

Every process should reduce ambiguity or improve decisions — nothing else. The moment a process becomes a box to check, it starts consuming the energy it was meant to generate. I cut anything that served reporting over thinking.

05

Culture follows what you celebrate

Teams optimize for whatever gets recognized. Celebrating early escalation and data-driven course correction — not just delivery — fundamentally changed how teams behaved when things went sideways. Which they always do.

07 — Honest Reflection

What I'd
Do Differently

Involve clinical stakeholders earlier in OKR design

We co-created OKRs with product, engineering, and leadership — but clinical validation came later. Earlier involvement from clinical advisors would have stress-tested the outcome framing and produced even sharper key results from the start.

Instrument the model before rollout, not after

Telemetry and analytics were integrated during rollout rather than before. Having a baseline measurement of what "before" looked like would have made the "after" metrics more defensible and the ROI story cleaner for leadership.

Move faster on manager enablement

The OKR framework worked well at the portfolio level. Where it lagged was at the team level — some engineering managers needed more support translating portfolio OKRs into their sprint planning. I'd invest in that enablement layer earlier.

Document the decision principles, not just the decisions

I documented what we decided but not always why we decided it that way. As teams scaled and new members joined, the absence of documented reasoning made some principles feel like arbitrary rules rather than deliberate choices.
