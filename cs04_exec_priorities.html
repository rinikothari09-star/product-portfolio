01 — The Situation

Two Valid
Agendas.
One Team.

As we scaled the cloud Enterprise Viewer, two executive factions formed — not out of bad intent, but out of legitimate business pressure. Each was right. That was the problem.

Sales leadership

"We need visible innovation — now."

The cloud viewer was our competitive differentiator. To win strategic deals and defend pipeline, Sales needed a fast-moving roadmap with differentiated capabilities they could take to market and demo convincingly to CIOs.

Optimising for Pipeline velocity · Competitive win rate · Market positioning
Operations & Platform leadership

"We can't sustain two parallel stacks."

Every week the legacy viewers stayed live was another week of mounting operational cost — incident load, maintenance overhead, engineering drag. The modernisation couldn't succeed while the old world kept demanding attention.

Optimising for Cost reduction · Operational simplicity · Technical debt reduction
The friction

Priorities were being debated in isolation — no shared view of trade-offs, no agreed sequencing, no portfolio-level frame

The cost

Teams were thrashing between signals — sprint planning changed based on which exec had spoken most recently

The risk

Neither priority would move meaningfully if both were treated as equal and simultaneous without structure

The trap

Choosing one agenda over the other would fracture exec trust and create a losing side — with real consequences

02 — The Reframe

Not Either/Or.
Sequencing.

The debate was framed as innovation vs. parity. That framing was the problem. I reframed it as a portfolio-level growth-vs-drag equation — and from there, the path forward became clear.

The question isn't which priority matters more. The question is: what is the true cost of the drag we're carrying, and what is the true leverage of the growth we're chasing — and can we sequence them so both happen?

— The reframe I brought to the executive conversation

The key insight: Innovation and consolidation were not in competition — they were in sequence. Every week spent maintaining legacy viewers was engineering capacity stolen from the cloud roadmap. Making that cost visible changed the conversation from "which matters more" to "how fast can we consolidate so growth can accelerate?"

What I mapped for Sales

Quantified which innovation requests had direct pipeline impact vs. which were nice-to-have — separating signal from noise in the roadmap ask

What I mapped for Ops

Quantified the true cost of legacy support — maintenance load, incident rate, engineering drag — in terms leadership could act on

What I established

Clear decision principles so trade-offs wouldn't be re-litigated every quarter from first principles

What I built

A dual-track execution model that gave each exec faction a visible, owned lane — with explicit guardrails on each

03 — The Model

The Dual-Track
Model

Every initiative in the portfolio had to declare which track it belonged to — and could only be prioritised within that track's guardrails. No more competing in the same queue.

The two tracks
Track A

Market & Growth

Owned by Product + Sales. Directly supports active pipeline conversations or unlocks future revenue.
StoryView premium-tier capabilities and enterprise differentiation features.
AI and EMR integration work that strengthens competitive positioning.
New specialty expansion (pathology, cardiology) that opens adjacent revenue streams.
Track B

Consolidation & EOL

Owned by Product + Operations. Narrowly scoped to removing blockers to customer migration.
Feature parity work — only what's necessary to retire a legacy viewer, not feature-for-feature recreation.
Customer migration tooling and change management support.
Legacy viewer end-of-life sequencing — each retirement frees capacity back into Track A.
The guardrail
Every initiative declared which track it served and which enterprise metric it moved. If it couldn't answer both questions, it didn't get prioritised. Innovation requests that didn't support active Sales motions went to the backlog. Parity work that recreated legacy features without enabling migration got cut.
04 — How I Built It

Building the
Operating Model

01

Diagnosed what each exec was actually optimising for

Before proposing anything, I mapped the incentive structure behind each agenda. Sales was optimising for win rate and pipeline velocity — their comp, their credibility, their relationships were on the line. Ops was optimising for system stability and cost efficiency — they were absorbing the pain of every legacy incident. Understanding these incentives shaped every conversation that followed.

02

Made the true cost of legacy support visible

Operational drag is invisible until you quantify it. I worked with Engineering and Ops to express legacy support in terms that resonated at the executive level: engineering hours per week absorbed by legacy incidents, cloud roadmap velocity lost to context-switching, monthly cost of running parallel stacks. Once the numbers were visible, the consolidation argument became self-evident.

03

Established decision principles before the model

Before introducing the dual-track model, I got alignment on the principles that would govern it — so the framework felt like a logical consequence, not an arbitrary structure. The two key principles: innovation only when it directly serves Sales motions; parity only when it unblocks a specific customer migration. Everything else goes to the backlog.

04

Introduced the dual-track model as a sequencing solution, not a compromise

I was careful to position this not as "you both get half" — that framing invites both sides to feel shortchanged. The pitch was: "Both priorities move forward. Track A runs hot on innovation. Track B runs deliberately on consolidation. Each legacy retirement accelerates Track A by freeing engineering capacity. They're not competing — they're feeding each other."

05

Embedded both tracks into planning and exec reviews

The model only works if both tracks are visible in the same review. I built a single dashboard view showing progress across both — innovation shipped, pipeline supported, legacy viewers retired, cost saved. Execs stopped advocating for their track and started managing the portfolio. That was the real shift.

06 — Why It Worked

Why It
Worked

Principle 01

Diagnose incentives before proposing solutions

Every exec conflict has an incentive structure underneath it. Understanding what each side was actually being measured on — not just what they were asking for — made it possible to design a model that genuinely served both.

Principle 02

Make invisible costs visible before asking for change

Operational drag is politically weightless until it has a number. Quantifying the cost of legacy support in exec-legible terms — engineering hours, roadmap velocity, monthly overhead — changed it from a complaint into a business case.

Principle 03

Sequence instead of compromise

Compromise gives each side less than they want. Sequencing gives each side what they want — in an order that's sustainable. The dual-track model worked because it wasn't splitting the pie, it was growing it over time through disciplined consolidation.

Principle 04

Establish principles before the model

A framework without principles gets gamed. Getting alignment on decision criteria first — what counts as innovation, what counts as parity, what belongs in the backlog — meant the model didn't require executive input on every individual decision.

Principle 05

Make both tracks visible in the same room

The dual-track model only worked because both tracks appeared in the same executive review. When execs see both priorities progressing together, they stop advocating and start governing. That shift from advocacy to governance is where real alignment lives.

Principle 06

Build a repeatable model, not a one-time fix

The goal wasn't to resolve this particular conflict — it was to build a decision system that would handle the next one. Embedding the model into quarterly planning meant it became self-sustaining rather than dependent on a mediator every cycle.

07 — Honest Reflection

What I'd
Do Differently

Introduce the model earlier in the scaling curve

By the time I formalised the dual-track model, significant thrash had already accumulated. The structural conditions that made it necessary — parallel stacks, competing exec agendas, unclear prioritisation — were visible earlier. I'd intervene before the pain becomes acute, not after.

Get Commercial ops involved in the EOL sequencing

Customer migration timelines were driven by product and engineering logic, but the commercial implications — contract timing, renewal windows, customer readiness — needed more input from the commercial side earlier. That would have made the EOL schedule more realistic and created fewer surprises for the account team.

Publish the decision principles more broadly

The principles that governed the dual-track model were understood at the exec and PM level, but didn't always reach engineering teams clearly. Broader publication would have reduced the number of escalations where someone asked "which track does this belong to?" mid-sprint.

Instrument track-level health metrics separately

Progress was tracked at the portfolio level but not always at the track level. Separate health metrics for Track A (pipeline supported, features shipped) and Track B (customers migrated, viewers retired) would have made the model more self-governing and easier to defend in exec reviews.
