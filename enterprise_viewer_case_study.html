02 — The Real Problem

The Problem
Behind the
Problem

The obvious answer was to build a better viewer. That wasn't the real problem. After 150+ clinical discovery interviews, a different picture emerged.

Clinicians don't think in studies or modalities. They think in patient stories. The problem wasn't tooling — it was a cognitive workflow gap that no one had named yet.

— Discovery insight, 150+ interviews with hospitalists, intensivists, surgeons, trauma teams, APPs
Root cause 01

Non-imaging clinicians couldn't make sense of a patient's longitudinal imaging history — dozens or hundreds of exams with no narrative thread

Root cause 02

Complex cases triggered repeated radiologist interruptions — not because clinicians lacked skill, but because they lacked the right context

Root cause 03

The imaging experience was designed for radiology-level expertise — entirely inaccessible to the broader care team

Root cause 04

No product in the market had reframed imaging as a shared clinical communication tool — the category was wide open

The reframe: This wasn't a viewer problem. It was a cognitive load problem. The question became: How do we help any clinician quickly understand the patient's imaging story over time — without requiring radiology expertise?

03 — My Approach

How I
Built It

End-to-end ownership — from defining the clinical problem to shipping, commercializing, and scaling. Six parallel tracks running simultaneously.

01

Deep clinical discovery before a single line of code

Conducted 150+ interviews across hospitalists, intensivists, trauma teams, surgeons, and APPs. Focused not on features but on how clinicians reason about imaging in real scenarios — where cognitive friction appeared and why. Insights shaped the entire product direction.

02

Defined the north star and invented StoryView

Translated discovery into a clear intended-use statement. Led cross-functional design using a design-thinking approach: lightweight prototypes, rapid clinician validation, focused MVP scoping. The result was StoryView — a longitudinal imaging narrative that became our trademark differentiator.

03

Built the cross-functional delivery model

Aligned 20+ engineers with matrixed UX, clinical SMEs, operations, compliance, and commercial functions around a single quality bar. Ran weekly workflow reviews on inter-study linking, diagnostic navigation, latency thresholds, and comparison logic — ensuring design reflected clinical thinking, not PACS conventions.

04

Secured additional UX resources and raised the bar

Pushed for enterprise-grade quality and secured the UX investment to back it up. Drove consistency across modalities — radiology, cardiology, pathology, patient access — so the experience felt unified regardless of specialty context.

05

Built the commercialization plan from the ground up

Repositioned the viewer as an enterprise asset, not a feature. Built outcome-based messaging aligned to CIO and CMIO priorities. Packaged StoryView as a premium capability for enterprise customers — anchoring upsell and renewal conversations. Partnered with sales and clinical adoption teams on rollout playbooks and pilot strategy.

06

Drove structured adoption at enterprise scale

Designed targeted pilot programs, enterprise rollout playbooks, and patient education paths. Ensured we could demonstrate measurable workflow impact — not just adoption numbers — to support renewal and expansion conversations.

Signature invention

StoryView™

StoryView collapsed a patient's entire imaging history into a single, longitudinal narrative. Instead of hunting through scattered exams, any clinician could see the patient's imaging journey — curated key images tied to the condition being treated, with reports automatically surfaced at the impression level.

Example: A hospitalist managing a TBI patient with 250+ imaging exams could see, in seconds, how the condition had evolved — without opening each study, without calling radiology. Reports highlighted. Key images curated by condition. Decision context immediately available.

First-of-its-kind · Trademark registered · Premium enterprise tier

04 — Key Decisions

Where I
Chose

The decisions that defined the product — and what I was willing to say no to.

Decision 01

Reframe the problem before building anything

There was pressure to move fast and build a "better viewer." I pushed back and invested first in deep discovery — 150+ interviews before committing to a direction. That investment surfaced the cognitive workflow gap that became StoryView. Without it, we'd have built a technically superior tool that solved the wrong problem.

Decision 02

Deprioritize feature breadth to nail one category-defining workflow

We had a long list of incremental asks from existing customers. I deliberately said no to most of them in the MVP phase to protect focus on StoryView. That meant short-term friction with some stakeholders — but it's what gave us a differentiated flagship rather than a feature-complete commodity.

Decision 03

Expand scope to include patients and the full care team — not just radiology

The safe play was building for radiologists — our established base. I made the call to design for the full care team from day one, which increased complexity but unlocked a fundamentally different market position. It also led directly to patient-facing features that opened new adoption vectors.

Decision 04

Align incentives around shared outcomes, not org chart lines

StoryView required engineering, UX, clinical SMEs, and commercial teams to row together — none of whom reported to me. I aligned everyone around the same north-star question: does this help a hospitalist make a faster, better-informed decision? That shared language replaced hierarchy as the alignment mechanism.

Decision 05

Pull back when over-engineering threatened clinical usability

Multiple times during development, I had to interrupt technically elegant solutions that were drifting from clinical reality. The hardest part of 0→1 work isn't building — it's continuously pulling the team back to the user's actual mental model and resisting the pull of architectural elegance for its own sake.

06 — Why It Worked

Why It
Worked

The viewer succeeded not because of the technology — but because of how we approached the problem. These are the operating principles that carried the work.

01

Start with cognitive load, not feature lists

Discovery focused on where and why clinicians lost context — not what features they wanted. That distinction is everything in healthcare product. Users rarely ask for the right thing; they describe the symptom of the problem they actually have.

02

Name the problem no one has named yet

Reframing the issue as a "cognitive workflow gap" gave everyone — engineering, commercial, clinical — a shared language to rally around. The reframe is often the product strategy.

03

Influence without authority through shared outcomes

Nothing in this build went smoothly through org-chart lines. Alignment came from relentlessly connecting each team's work to one question: does this help a clinician make a faster, better-informed decision? That question replaced hierarchy.

04

Protect the north star from incremental erosion

The biggest risk in 0→1 work isn't technical failure — it's slow drift back to familiar solutions. I had to continuously re-anchor the team to clinical reality and resist technically elegant but clinically meaningless design decisions.

05

Build the commercial story in parallel, not after

GTM was not a post-launch activity. The StoryView premium-tier packaging, the CIO and CMIO messaging, the enterprise rollout playbooks — all of it was designed alongside the product. That's why adoption scaled in year one instead of plateauing.

07 — Honest Reflection

What I'd
Do Differently

Move faster on GTM alignment

The product and commercial tracks were mostly sequential rather than truly parallel in the early phases. Earlier, deeper integration with sales would have accelerated enterprise pipeline and reduced the time between launch and meaningful ARR.

Invest in patient-facing earlier

Patient adoption became a meaningful growth lever — but it was treated as secondary to clinician workflows initially. In hindsight, the patient use case was differentiated enough to warrant a parallel track from day one.

Instrument adoption metrics sooner

We had strong top-line adoption numbers, but granular feature-level telemetry lagged. Earlier instrumentation would have accelerated StoryView iteration and given us sharper clinical outcome data to anchor enterprise renewals.

Document the decision architecture

The strategic decisions that shaped this product lived in my head and in meeting notes. A cleaner decision log would have accelerated onboarding of new team members and preserved institutional context as the team scaled.
