01 — The Situation

The
Standoff

I was leading a complex feature spanning three products and their interfaces. It required a new back-end utility to update historical data in the archive. Engineering built it as a CLI tool — clean, functional, appropriate for the scope. Then Operations drew a hard line.

The conflict
Operations

No GUI, no go-live.

CLI-only means manual configuration, error-prone scripting, and unsustainable support overhead. We won't back this in production.

vs.
Commercial / Product

A full GUI adds 4 months.

20+ customers are waiting. Contractual commitments are in play. We cannot afford to slip a full release cycle.

At stake

20+ customer go-lives blocked — each with commercial milestones tied to this feature

The risk

Slipping a full release cycle would push revenue recognition out of the quarter

The constraint

Both sides had valid, non-negotiable positions — this wasn't a misunderstanding, it was a genuine trade-off

The trap

Forcing a decision without resolution would leave one team unsupported in production or the other holding a broken commercial commitment

02 — My Approach

Reframe
Before
Resolving

The first move wasn't to propose a solution. It was to change the question. "GUI or no GUI" was a positional debate. The real question was: what does Operations actually need to feel safe supporting this in production?

01

Made the full impact visible to everyone in the same room

I pulled Operations, Services, Engineering, and the Account team into one working session — not separate conversations. Before opening the floor, I shared a single one-page view: number of customers blocked, revenue at risk, GUI build effort vs. current CLI utility. The goal was to reframe this from "Product vs. Ops" to a shared business problem that nobody wanted.

02

Unpacked the real needs behind Operations' hard line

I asked Operations to walk through their worst-case scenario step by step: where do errors happen, how many customers would need the utility run, what skills does the support team have or lack? What emerged was precise: their concerns weren't about the CLI itself — they were about misconfiguration risk, lack of auditability, and per-customer run time. That reframe changed the problem from "we need a GUI" to "we need safety, repeatability, and low overhead."

03

Worked with Engineering to find the minimum viable safety net

Separate from the joint session, I worked with Engineering to identify what could be delivered in four to six weeks that would directly address Operations' real concerns without building a full GUI. We identified four levers: parameter validation and dry-run mode, standardised config templates by customer profile, structured logs ingestible into monitoring tools, and a slim internal console covering the top three use cases only.

04

Presented three concrete options — not a recommendation in disguise

I brought everyone back with three fully specified scenarios, each with effort, risk, and business impact laid out. The goal was to make the trade-off visible, not to pre-sell an answer. When the options are clear, the right path usually becomes obvious without anyone needing to be convinced.

05

De-risked the phased approach specifically for Operations

To get Operations comfortable with Phase 1, I proposed concrete safeguards: pilot with three early adopter customers with white-glove support, require rollback capability on every run, include Operations in the design review for the slim console, and commit to tracking actual effort and error rates in a monthly Ops-Product review. The full GUI was time-boxed into the next major release with firm dates.

06

Documented the decision and coordinated external messaging

Once alignment was reached, I documented the decision, risks, and mitigation plan and took it to leadership for sign-off. I then worked with the commercial team to prepare customer messaging — setting honest expectations about the initial release scope while confirming the timeline held.

03 — The Decision

Three Options.
One Path.

I never walked into a conflict resolution with one answer. I came with three — fully specified, with trade-offs made explicit. This moved the conversation from debate to decision.

Option A

Full GUI now

+Ideal Operations experience from day one
+No phased rollout complexity
—4-month delay to all customer go-lives
—Significant revenue slips out of quarter
—Engineering capacity consumed by internal tooling
Option B

CLI only, ship now

+Maintains current timeline
+No additional engineering work
—High operational risk and manual overhead
—Operations cannot support safely at scale
—Not acceptable — would create production incidents
Option C

Phased approach

+Timeline maintained — ~6 weeks from original target
+Operations' real concerns addressed directly
+Full GUI ships next release with real usage data
+Revenue secured in the committed quarter
—Requires tight execution and early adopter white-glove
Chosen

Phase 1 (3 weeks): Hardened CLI with parameter validation, dry-run mode, config templates per customer profile, structured logs, and a slim internal console covering the top 3 use cases. Piloted with 3 early adopter customers.

Phase 2 (next major release): Full GUI incorporating real usage patterns from Phase 1 pilots. Time-boxed with firm dates. Operations included in design reviews throughout.

04 — How It Played Out

Week by
Week

The phased approach wasn't just a plan — it was a structured de-risking exercise. Here's how it unfolded.

Week 1–2

Joint working session + options alignment

Ops, Engineering, Services, and Commercial in one room. One-pager shared. Positions unpacked into needs. Three options presented. Option C agreed. Decision documented and sent to leadership for sign-off.

Week 3–5

Phase 1 build — hardened CLI + slim console

Engineering delivered parameter validation, dry-run mode, config templates, structured logs, and the slim internal console. Operations reviewed the console design. Three early adopter customers identified and prepped for pilot.

Week 6

Early adopter pilot with white-glove support

Feature launched to three pilot customers. Support effort per customer tracked. Actual run time and error rate monitored. Findings fed directly into Phase 2 GUI design scope.

Week 6 onward

20+ customer go-lives unblocked

Feature rolled out to the full waiting customer base. Monthly Ops-Product review established. Phase 2 full GUI scoped and time-boxed into the next major release train.

Next major release

Phase 2 full GUI ships — better than planned

The full GUI launched with real-world usage patterns from pilot customers informing the design. More focused, better prioritised, and more closely aligned to actual support workflows than the original spec would have been.

06 — Why It Worked

Why It
Worked

The resolution wasn't clever. It was disciplined. These are the principles that made it possible.

01

Positions are symptoms — needs are the diagnosis

"We need a GUI" was a position. The underlying need was safety, repeatability, and low overhead. Resolving the position was impossible. Addressing the need was straightforward. This reframe is the entire skill — and most stakeholder conflicts never get there because nobody asks the second question.

02

Bring everyone into the same room with the same facts

Bilateral conversations let each side maintain their position. A joint session with shared impact data changed the dynamic — suddenly everyone was looking at the same customer list, the same revenue number, the same engineering estimate. Shared facts create shared accountability.

03

Options, not recommendations

Walking in with one answer puts you in the position of selling. Walking in with three — fully specified, with trade-offs explicit — puts you in the position of facilitating. The latter is faster and produces more durable alignment because people feel like they chose, not like they were convinced.

04

De-risk the path you want — don't just argue for it

I could have presented the phased approach and asked Operations to trust it. Instead, I built the safeguards they needed into the proposal itself — pilots, rollback capability, design involvement, monthly reviews. The ask became: "will you try this?" not "do you believe this will work?"

05

Document and control the narrative

Alignment in a room disappears without documentation. I wrote up the decision, the risks, and the mitigation plan, took it to leadership for sign-off, and then coordinated the external customer messaging. Every step of the resolution was deliberate — including what got communicated to whom and when.

07 — Honest Reflection

What I'd
Do Differently

Surface the dependency earlier

The Operations concern was foreseeable — any CLI tool running at enterprise scale against production data should have triggered a conversation earlier in the design process. I'd build a pre-launch operations review into the standard feature checklist for anything touching production data.

Quantify the support burden upfront

The impact assessment I prepared for the joint session took time to build under pressure. A standard template for quantifying operational risk — customers affected, run time, skill requirements, error surface — would have made this faster and more reusable across similar decisions.

Pilot structure planned before the conflict, not after

The early adopter pilot was designed reactively as a de-risking mechanism. In hindsight, a structured pilot would have been the right approach regardless — for feature validation, not just stakeholder management. Building it into the launch plan from the start would have been cleaner.

Convert the resolution into a repeatable playbook sooner

This became an internal reference model informally. I should have written it up as a formal playbook immediately after — while the decisions were fresh. The institutional memory faded faster than it should have as team membership changed.
